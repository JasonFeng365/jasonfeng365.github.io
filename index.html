About Me


This is a picture from Sierra College's 2022 Club Day.

I started attending Sierra College as a full-time student in the summer of 2021. In early spring 2022, I was approached by Sierra College's Young Developers club, who were looking for officers to lead the club.
While I first joined as a standard officer, I quickly rose to president by the beginning of summer, and led lectures during club meetings, lecturing on various algorithms, techniques, and puzzles, along with encouraging my classmates to participate in programming competitions.
Spring 2023 is my last semester here. I will transfer to a University of California with an associate's degree, and pursue my graduate degree at university.

My programming pathway was a long one, with a rough start. My first language was HTML, of which I learned the basics at home. I later attended a summer course over the summer of my fourth grade. While I know many bright students who have good understanding of programming at a young age, I was not one of these. I despised the class, completely shutting myself away from HTML, and it took me two years until I began to code again.
Now in the sixth grade, my friend introduced me to Scratch, which was my first exposure to writing dynamic programs, rather than static HTML pages. I enjoyed moving at my own pace, rather than a fast-moving week-long course, and I made many games, mostly platformers, inspired by the projects my friends have made. While I liked makes games with drag-and-drop code, typed code was still difficult to me, and I exclusively coded using Scratch until high school.
I took an information technology class in my freshman years. Here, I learned a lot about computer hardware, but I had not began coding yet. It was not until my sophomore year, when I took the second information technology class, that I began typing code again. Although I had no real experience writing code, by the end of the year, I was the farthest in the self-paced curriculum in the entire class.
The COVID-19 pandemic began in my junior year. Due to this, I had a lot of time, and decided to prepare for my next school year by learning Java. I took an online class from Udemy, and my interest in code bloomed as I solved puzzle after puzzle and created project after project. Eventually, I spent two months during my last high school semester studying tirelessly to become Oracle certified, and reached my goal on April 8, 2021. To this day, Java remains my favorite language.

Despite all that I've learned from school, my greatest opportunity has been working with Rocklin's Code Ninjas team. I first joined the team in the fall of 2021, as a recommendation from my friend. Here in the code dojo, I learned more about competitive programming, along with the way of thinking needed to problem-solve effectively.
If school and my courses began my path in programming, Code Ninjas only added to my momentum.

My journey in competitive programming began in high school, where I participated in HPE CodeWars in Spring 2021, my senior year. At the time, I had used Java for slightly less than a year, and teamed up with my friends, two other Java users, to compete in CodeWars. Although we scored only around average, this was my first exposure to competitive programming.
I learned even more about competitions from my manager at Code Ninjas. My coworkers, my manager, and I partook in many competitions, including the Google Code Jam and the Meta Hacker Cup. Besides competitions, we explored algorithms by solving various problems from Project Euler, Codility, and similar sites.

My greatest advice for computer science students is to build some projects outside of school. Courses in school will teach you knowledge and theory, but you will have to find the passion to apply that knowledge by yourself- you should never depend completely on school for becoming a programmer.
Take some self-paced online courses, and completely master one programming language- learning how to think like a programmer is far more important than learning different languages. Work on some personal projects- a to-do list is a very common project to begin with. While that is completely fine, it is infinitely better to find a project you are interested in. For me, that is Discord bots and automation. For you, it may be a game, a utility, an algorithm, or something else altogether.
Regardless, find something that you are interested in, just as I have, and you will blaze ahead on your path as a programmer.

Fours Puzzle

I participated in 2022's Meta Hacker Cup, making my way through the qualification and first rounds. On the night of September 18th, I dreamed of a problem that would appear on Round 2: a puzzle involving the number 4, and four mathematical operations. Unfortunately, it didn't actually appear on the contest!

Given a finite amount of the number 4, and the mathematical operations +, -, *, and ÷, determine whether it is possible or not to produce a result using those operations.

Input will be formatted as follows. The first line will be an integer number of how many inputs there will be. For each input, there will be two positive integers N and K separated by a space. The first integer N is how many 4’s you are allowed to use. The second integer K is the number to calculate.
1 <= N <= 10
0 <= K <= 1,000,000

Example input:
3 2
5 1
3 10
1 0

In Case 1, we are given three 4s to get the number 2 as a result. We can do: (4 + 4) / 4 to return the number 2.
In Case 2, we are given five 4s to get the number 1. We can do: 4 / 4 to return the number 1. The number of 4s we use does not have to be exactly N, but has to be less or equal to N.
In Case 3, we are given three 4s to get 10. 10 can be found with 4 + 4 + (4 + 4) / 4; however, we print NO because we used more than three 4s.
In Case 4, with only one 4, our only possible result is 4. Therefore, we cannot get a result of 0.

The answer of this puzzle is the sum of the valid inputs' case numbers.
For example, for the example input, the first and second inputs are valid, but the third and fourth are not, so I would add 1 and 2 to get a solution of 3.

Power-up Maze

I dreamed of the basic premise of this puzzle on the 24th of December, after reading about a puzzle with (possibly) a similar solution. I later added onto it, while I was awake.

A mouse is running through a maze, represented by a 2D grid, shown below. A # represents a wall, and a . represents an empty tile. Each second, it can move one tile upwards, leftwards, rightwards, or downwards on an empty space, but cannot go through walls. The mouse starts on the space marked A, and wants to move to the space marked B.

Around the maze are one or more powerups marked P. The mouse has to collect every tile marked P in order to complete the maze. Whenever the mouse moves over a space marked P, it can choose to collect the powerup. If it does, the space becomes an empty tile, and the mouse's current second ends. On subsequent moves, the mouse is able to move an additional tile for every powerup collected.

Given a maze, return the least amount of time the mouse takes to reach point B, starting from point A.
